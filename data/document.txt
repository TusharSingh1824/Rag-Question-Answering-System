The Solar System: A Comprehensive Overview

The Solar System consists of the Sun and the celestial objects that are bound to it by gravity.
These objects include eight planets, their moons, dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, and meteoroids.
The Solar System formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a giant molecular cloud.
The vast majority of the system's mass is in the Sun, with most of the remaining mass contained in Jupiter.

The Inner Planets

The four inner planets, also known as terrestrial or rocky planets, are Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars.
These planets are composed primarily of rock and metal and have solid surfaces.
Mercury is the smallest planet and closest to the Sun, with extreme temperature variations from -180°C to 430°C.
Venus is the hottest planet in the Solar System due to its thick carbon dioxide atmosphere creating a runaway greenhouse effect.
Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet to support life, with liquid water covering about 71% of its surface.
Mars, often called the Red Planet due to iron oxide on its surface, has the largest volcano in the Solar System called Olympus Mons.

The Outer Planets

The four outer planets, also called gas giants and ice giants, are Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune.
Jupiter is the largest planet in the Solar System, with a mass more than twice that of all other planets combined.
Jupiter's Great Red Spot is a massive storm larger than Earth that has been continuously observed for over 350 years.
Saturn is best known for its spectacular ring system, which is made primarily of ice particles, rocky debris, and dust.
Uranus is unique among the planets because it rotates on its side, with an axial tilt of approximately 98 degrees.
Neptune is the farthest known planet from the Sun and experiences the strongest winds of any planet, reaching up to 2,100 km/h.

Dwarf Planets and Small Bodies

Pluto was reclassified from a planet to a dwarf planet in 2006 by the International Astronomical Union (IAU).
The five officially recognized dwarf planets are Pluto, Eris, Haumea, Makemake, and Ceres.
Ceres is the largest object in the asteroid belt located between Mars and Jupiter.
The asteroid belt contains millions of rocky objects, but its total mass is less than 4% of the Moon's mass.
Comets are icy bodies that develop visible tails when they approach the Sun and their ice begins to sublimate.

The Sun

The Sun is a G-type main-sequence star (G2V) that makes up about 99.86% of the total mass of the Solar System.
Its surface temperature is approximately 5,500 degrees Celsius, while its core reaches about 15 million degrees Celsius.
The Sun generates energy through nuclear fusion, converting roughly 600 million tons of hydrogen into helium every second.
Light from the Sun takes approximately 8 minutes and 20 seconds to reach Earth, traveling at 299,792 km per second.
The Sun is currently about 4.6 billion years old and is roughly halfway through its main-sequence life stage.
In about 5 billion years, the Sun will exhaust its hydrogen fuel and expand into a red giant star.

Moons and Natural Satellites

There are over 200 known natural satellites orbiting planets in our Solar System.
Earth has one natural satellite, commonly known as the Moon, which has a significant effect on Earth's tides.
Jupiter has 95 known moons, including the four large Galilean moons discovered by Galileo in 1610: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto.
Europa is of particular scientific interest because evidence suggests it has a subsurface ocean beneath its icy crust that could potentially harbor life.
Saturn has 146 confirmed moons, with Titan being the largest and the only moon in the Solar System with a substantial atmosphere.
Titan's surface features lakes and rivers of liquid methane and ethane, making it a unique world in our Solar System.

Space Exploration Milestones

The Space Age began on October 4, 1957, when the Soviet Union launched Sputnik 1, the first artificial satellite.
Yuri Gagarin became the first human to journey into outer space on April 12, 1961, aboard Vostok 1.
The Apollo 11 mission successfully landed the first humans on the Moon on July 20, 1969, with Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin.
NASA's Voyager 1, launched in 1977, became the first human-made object to enter interstellar space in 2012.
The Hubble Space Telescope, deployed in 1990, has made over 1.5 million observations and revolutionized our understanding of the universe.
Mars exploration has been a major focus, with rovers like Curiosity (2012) and Perseverance (2021) searching for signs of ancient microbial life.
The James Webb Space Telescope, launched on December 25, 2021, is the most powerful space telescope ever built and observes the universe in infrared.
